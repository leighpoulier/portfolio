Lake Titicaca, Peru/Bolivia

Posted by Leigh on 13 Jan 2016

Today i had organised another day tour, this time to Lake Titicaca, approx 400km south east of Cusco. Lake Titicaca is the highest navigable lake in the world at 3800m and covers 8560 sq.km, roughly half of it is in Peru and half Bolivia.

It’s pronounced Ti-ti-kha-kha, a pre-incan word meaning ‘grey puma’ because if you look at it from above with south at the top it apparently looks like one. I would like to know what kind of space craft these ancient civilisations invented to be able to view everything from the sky!

To get to Lake Titicaca involved a 6.5 hour overnight coach trip, departing Cusco around 1030pm bound for Puno, on the western bank of the lake. The coach was designed for overnight travel, with larger seats, only 3 seats per row, with greater recline. Kinda like premium economy on an aeroplane.

I would have much preferred to be on an aeroplane though, as there would have been much less turbulence. The road felt like we were driving cross country, with the driver purposely trying to find the most deeply rutted, vibration inducing path possible. I could barely sleep at all, but even with my eyes rattling inside my head i think i might have passed out through complete exhaustion at 2am, only to have the ugly lights turned on at 4am for an intermediate stop.

At 5am we arrived at Puno’s Terminale Terestre, and i staggered into the terminal wanting to just die.

By 7am i was out on a boat, completely underdressed for the 6 degrees on the lake. The first stop on the tour was the Islas Uros, which was the real reason I wanted to visit the Lake Titicaca. Islas Uros are a community of floating islands made from the reeds that grow in the shallows of the lake.

The people who build and live on these islands were formerly an ethnically separate group who took to the water to avoid conflict with competing land based groups centuries ago.

The island is like a large water bed; when you walk across it your feet sink in and the surface of the island flexes. The reeds are replenished on top as they decompose underwater from the bottom.

There are about 40 such islands on the lake and every day a flotilla of tourist boats visit, so this has become their main income. With synthetic ropes now used in the islands construction, and solar power panels providing electricity,it seems the modern world is eroding the uniqueness of this way of life.

Next we continued to Isla Taquile, an island in the middle of the lake which was quite isolated until the 70s when the resident community pooled their resources and bought a motor boat.

The island was first settled in pre-incan times and exhibits the typical terraced hillsides that the Inca adopted and improved. We climbed over the island from one side to the other, reaching 4050m in the process, and witnessed the locals unique dress and ways of life. No photos of the locals because i wasn’t feeling very generous after my last ‘voluntario’ experience!

The weather vastly improved during the afternoon and the journey back was relaxing and of course i fell asleep.
